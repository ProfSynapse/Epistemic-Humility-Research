## Abstract

Language models acquire most of their epistemic character (how confident they sound, when they refuse, how readily they capitulate) not from pretraining but from post-training. We present a systematic evidence synthesis of how training interventions create, damage, and partially repair epistemic humility in large language models. From six structured literature searches plus a backward-citation pass over the full bibliography (June 2026; 110 documented queries) we extracted 75 quantitative effect rows from 38 studies into a unified schema spanning calibration, abstention, hallucination, sycophancy, and method comparisons. Because the literature almost never reports variance (no error bars were found in any retrieved material from the twelve calibration studies in our primary searches), we synthesize by vote counting with exact binomial sign tests and descriptive normalization rather than formal pooling, reading the sign tests descriptively; only one family reaches conventional significance. Five claim families emerge: post-training degrades token-probability calibration; preference-based methods beat supervised fine-tuning (SFT) on abstention and truthfulness quality in every extracted comparison but one (the IPO variant underperforms); preference optimization reduces SFT-induced over-refusal (which our independent reanalysis of released model outputs confirms exactly: over-refusal on known questions falls from 42.71% under SFT to 23.27% under direct preference optimization (DPO), n = 11,313); scale alone does not produce humility (in our reanalysis of AbstentionBench's released results, 50× more parameters moves abstention recall by 0.02 while a single DPO stage moves it 0.08); and targeted interventions reliably help (sign test p = 0.002). Organizing the evidence by a four-level hierarchy we call the Depths of Ignorance reveals that nearly all existing measurement sits at the shallowest depth, scalar confidence, and that an unreconciled tension (preference methods improve abstention while damaging calibration) plus several verified method gaps (no Kahneman-Tversky optimization (KTO) abstention study; no SFT/DPO/KTO three-way; no probe-transfer test of whether humility training changes representations or only behavior) define the open frontier.

## 1. Introduction

A language model that answers fluently when it knows and abstains gracefully when it does not would be, in a precise sense, aligned: its expressed epistemic state would track its actual one. The models we deploy are not like this. They are well documented to assert falsehoods with high verbal confidence, to refuse questions they could answer, and to abandon correct answers under trivial social pressure. A recent theoretical account gives this failure mode a name we adopt as the framing for this synthesis: the **polite liar** [arXiv:2511.07477], a system that structurally misrepresents its own epistemic state, not out of anything resembling malice, but because its training rewarded the appearance of knowledge over the admission of ignorance. The name earns its keep through what it refuses to blame: the liar is polite, eager to help, and doing exactly what it was taught. The contention of this synthesis is that these failures are not primarily facts about model scale or architecture. They are facts about *training*, specifically post-training, and the evidence for that claim is now broad enough, and quantitative enough, to be synthesized systematically.

Three strands of evidence converge on training as the causal locus.

**First, pretrained models already know how likely they are to be right; post-training breaks the readout.** A model is *calibrated* when its probabilities are honest forecasts of its own accuracy: across all the answers to which it assigns roughly 90% confidence, it should be correct roughly 90% of the time. Calibration is not accuracy: a model can be wrong often and still calibrated, so long as it is unconfident when it is wrong. Expected calibration error (ECE) measures the failure of this property: group the model's answers into bins by confidence, compare each bin's average confidence to its actual accuracy, and average the gaps; zero is perfect [arXiv:1706.04599]. By this standard, pretrained models are strikingly well calibrated. The GPT-4 technical report measures an ECE of 0.007 for the pretrained base model on a subset of MMLU (Massive Multitask Language Understanding) [arXiv:2009.03300]: when the base model said 85%, it was right about 85% of the time, despite next-token prediction never explicitly teaching it to estimate its own reliability. After reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF), ECE on the same subset rises tenfold to 0.074 [arXiv:2303.08774] (both ECE values are read from the report's calibration figure, Figure 8; its body text states only the direction): the same model, on the same questions, now states confidences that systematically overshoot its accuracy. Crucially, the damage is shallow. Kadavath et al. report that RLHF policies "naively appear very miscalibrated," identify the mechanism (RLHF concentrates probability mass on high-reward outputs, sharpening every distribution whether or not the model's knowledge warrants it), and show that a single temperature adjustment (one global constant that uniformly softens the probabilities without changing their ranking) largely restores calibration [arXiv:2207.05221]. That repairability is itself evidence: if post-training had *erased* the model's self-knowledge, no one-parameter rescaling could bring it back. The signal survives in the weights; it is merely expressed too confidently.

**Second, the damage is not specific to reinforcement learning (RL).** A controlled comparison on the same base model finds plain instruction tuning (supervised fine-tuning on instruction-response pairs, with no reward model anywhere) nearly tripling ECE (0.13 → 0.36) while simultaneously *reducing* predictive entropy (1.32 → 0.92) [arXiv:2509.20088]. Predictive entropy measures how spread out the model's probabilities are: lower entropy means the model commits harder to its top answer. The two numbers moving in opposite directions is the signature of the problem: the tuned model becomes more decisive and less reliable about its own reliability at the same time, sharper and wronger at once.

**Third, the converse also holds: what training breaks, training can deliberately improve.** Interventions designed to target humility (refusal-aware tuning that teaches a model to say "I don't know" on questions it gets wrong, factuality-aware DPO, calibrated reward models, preference pairs scored by whether a listener is correctly persuaded) consistently improve humility metrics, often by large margins (Section 5.2, family C5). Training giveth and training taketh away.

The shape of the problem is old. In Plato's *Meno*, Socrates' geometry lesson leads an enslaved boy from confident error, through cross-examination, into the productive perplexity the Greeks called *aporia*, and only then toward recovered knowledge. On the evidence above, standard post-training runs that lesson backwards: it starts from a model that is, by the narrow token-probability standard, already past the confident-error stage, and teaches it overconfidence.

The stakes are concrete. Anthropic's analysis of human preference data found that matching the user's beliefs raises a response's selection probability by up to ~6%, placing it among the most predictive features of human preference, comparable to or exceeding truthfulness depending on the condition [arXiv:2310.13548]; preference datasets also reward face-preserving "social sycophancy" [arXiv:2505.13995]. A production incident made the mechanism vivid: OpenAI's April 2025 GPT-4o sycophancy regression was traced to new thumbs-up/down reward signals that "weakened the influence of our primary reward signal, which had been holding sycophancy in check" (OpenAI 2025a). The mechanism has since been demonstrated under controlled conditions: fine-tuning with RLHF against simulated immediate user satisfaction raises a model's Bullshit Index (one minus the correlation between its elicited belief and its stated claim) from 0.379 to 0.665 and pushes deceptive positive claims about unknown product attributes from 20.9% to 84.5% (Llama-3-8b for the claim rates; the paper's Bullshit-Index figure does not name the model explicitly) [arXiv:2507.07484]. When the optimization target itself prefers confident agreement, the polite liar of our framing is not a training failure but a training *success*: the posture is the optimum of the objective the model was actually given. Kalai et al. give the complementary incentive-level account: binary-graded training and evaluation reward guessing over acknowledging uncertainty, so models are "optimized to be good test-takers" [arXiv:2509.04664]; the theoretical companion result shows the bind precedes the incentives, since even a statistically calibrated pretrained model must hallucinate at some rate on facts attested too rarely in its training data [arXiv:2311.14648].

What has been missing is a synthesis that (a) treats these literatures (calibration, abstention/IDK ("I don't know") tuning, hallucination, sycophancy, and the SFT-versus-preference-method comparisons) as one evidence base about a single underlying construct; (b) is honest about the verification status of every number it reports; and (c) identifies which experiments the field has *not* run, with enough precision that the gaps are falsifiable claims about the literature rather than vibes. This paper attempts all three. Requirement (b) is, we recognize, self-referential: a synthesis about training models to assert only what they can support is in no position to assert numbers it has not checked. We treat the parallel as an obligation rather than an ornament; where we could not verify, the paper abstains from the claim (Section 4.5), and Section 7 reports what that discipline caught in our own pipeline. Our contributions:

1. A unified extraction of 75 quantitative effects from 38 studies into a single schema (Section 4), synthesized by vote counting and exact binomial sign tests with explicit justification for why formal meta-analytic pooling is currently impossible (Section 4.4).
2. Independent reanalyses of three studies' released artifacts: an output-level reanalysis of Cheng et al.'s "Say I Don't Know" model outputs, yielding exact over-refusal and refusal-recall numbers the paper itself does not report; a paired reanalysis of AbstentionBench's released results table, yielding post-training-ladder and scale effects its paper does not report; and a descriptive reanalysis of FActScore's released generations (Section 5.3).
3. An organizing conceptual framework introduced here, the four-level Depths of Ignorance, plus a cross-cutting coherence/faithfulness axis, which together explain *where* the literature's measurements cluster and what the clustering hides (Sections 3, 5.5).
4. A verified gap analysis: six specific, unclaimed experiments, each stated as a falsifiable claim about absence (Section 6.3).

## 2. Scope and terminology

We use *epistemic humility* as an umbrella for behaviors and properties that make a model's expressed epistemic state track its actual reliability: token-probability and verbalized-confidence calibration; appropriate abstention ("I don't know") with low over-refusal; resistance to hallucination on unfamiliar inputs; and resistance to sycophantic capitulation. We use *post-training* for everything after pretraining: supervised/instruction fine-tuning (SFT); preference optimization, covering RLHF [arXiv:2203.02155] with proximal policy optimization (PPO) [arXiv:1707.06347], direct preference optimization (DPO) [arXiv:2305.18290], Kahneman-Tversky optimization (KTO) [arXiv:2402.01306], and best-of-n (BoN) selection against a reward model; and reasoning-oriented RL. Studies enter the synthesis if they report a quantitative effect of a training condition (or a training-targeted intervention) on at least one humility-relevant metric, or if they provide benchmark evidence needed to interpret such effects (Section 4.2).

## 3. Conceptual framework: the Depths of Ignorance plus a coherence axis

A synthesis needs an organizing taxonomy, and the natural one available is not a method taxonomy (the existing surveys of abstention [arXiv:2407.18418] and honesty [arXiv:2409.18786] already provide those) but a *depth* taxonomy: a hierarchy of what, exactly, is being formalized when a model "expresses ignorance." We organize the evidence by the four **Depths of Ignorance**, a hierarchy we introduce for this synthesis, ordered by how much structure the model's expressed ignorance carries:

- **L1: Confidence/calibration.** "How sure am I?" formalized as a scalar. The raw signals are token probabilities and verbalized percentages ("I am 90% sure"); the standard scores summarize how well those signals track reality. ECE (Section 1) measures whether the stated confidences match the actual hit rates. The Brier score is the mean squared error between stated confidence and the actual 0-or-1 outcome, penalizing confident misses hardest. The area under the receiver operating characteristic curve (AUROC) discards the absolute numbers and asks only how well confidence *ranks* answers: the probability that a randomly chosen correct answer received higher confidence than a randomly chosen incorrect one, where 0.5 is coin-flipping and 1.0 is a perfect ordering. This is the level of nearly all training work synthesized below.
- **L2: Structured ignorance.** "What am I missing?" formalized as structure: gap-naming, knowledge-intersection identification, retrieval proposals. This is the depth of genuine *aporia* in the *Meno*'s sense: not a flat "I don't know" but an articulated being-at-a-loss that names which belief failed and what would resolve it. The clearest instantiation is the Structured Ignorance Certificate, trained via RL on a 7,347-question cross-domain Unknown-Unknown dataset, with 99.46% of certificates emitted as valid JSON on 735 held-out questions [arXiv:2606.08571]; the theoretical complement decomposes uncertainty into input ambiguity, knowledge gaps, and aleatoric sampling noise [arXiv:2603.24967], which a scalar confidence collapses into one number.
- **L3: Distributional/third-person signatures.** "What shape is my failure?" formalized from outside the model: population-level features of repeated failed attempts that predict whether a failure is recoverable, steerable with scaffolding, or a genuine dead end [arXiv:2606.05145], with step-level trace scoring as a refinement [arXiv:2606.06475]. Here the model does not know what it does not know, but the distribution does.
- **L4: Objective uncertainty.** "What should I even be optimizing?" formalized as calibrated uncertainty over the reward itself: replacing a single fixed "good" with a distribution produces behavior that converges where humans agree and diversifies where they do not, performing as well as any single-objective model (and better when the reward judge is noisy) [arXiv:2606.03962]. L4 bears directly on sycophancy, which requires collapsing to the implicit objective "good = what this user wants right now."

To the depths we add one **cross-cutting axis: coherence/faithfulness.** At any level one can ask whether the model's *stated* epistemic signal, its *token-level* signal, and its *hidden-state* signal agree. Recent work gives this axis empirical teeth: the first systematic framework for "faithful calibration" finds that token-probability, hidden-state, and sampled-consistency estimators of internal confidence *diverge* on the same reasoning traces (estimator fragility), and that extended chain-of-thought reasoning does not improve the alignment between expressed confidence and internal state [arXiv:2606.03969]. Two independent groups corroborate the reasoning result: more inference-time reasoning consistently impairs calibration [arXiv:2508.15050], and reasoning models do not know when they don't know [arXiv:2506.18183]. Sycophancy, meanwhile, is steerable through an identifiable valence-arousal subspace of internal representations: arousal steering alone moves sycophancy between 61% and 84% on the Political Typology benchmark, replicating across Llama-3.1-8B and Qwen3-8B/14B [arXiv:2604.03147]: direct representation-level evidence that a humility-adjacent behavior lives in a readable internal direction. The same mechanistic lens is beginning to move upstream into post-training data itself: Bergen et al. use SAE/probe-derived concept audits of preference data to predict and partially mitigate off-target DPO learning, including safety degradation, sycophancy-like physics behavior, and hallucinated sensitive-resource links [arXiv:2606.12360]. The coherence axis is what distinguishes *possessed* humility from *performed* humility, and, as we show in Section 5.5, it is almost entirely unmeasured in the training literature. The *Meno* closes on this exact distinction: true opinions, Socrates says, are like the statues of Daedalus, which run away unless tethered by working out the reason. A humility behavior not anchored to the model's internal state is an untethered statue: the right answer today, a runaway under distribution shift. Further training can silently suppress reasoning traces while benchmark scores hide the damage (visible-reasoning rate collapses to 0% even as pass@1 improves by 50–57%) [arXiv:2605.21127]; the adjacent monitorability literature adds a boundary condition: only adversarial optimization that explicitly penalizes monitor accuracy consistently degrades chain-of-thought monitorability, while common training incentives show no consistent effect [arXiv:2512.00218].

This framework does descriptive work in Section 5 (the literature's metrics cluster at L1, and we can now say precisely how much) and prescriptive work in Section 6 (the highest-value open experiments live on the coherence axis and at L2-L3).

## 4. Methods

### 4.1 Search strategy

Evidence was gathered in June 2026 through five structured fan-out searches, one per evidence area, executed by independent large language model (LLM) search agents (Section 4.6 details the AI division of labor), plus one follow-up search (mechanistic-interpretability/probing and verifiable-RL literature for the coherence axis, feeding a sixth raw report). Per-report query counts are documented in each raw report's frontmatter: calibration-vs-RLHF (15 queries), abstention/IDK fine-tuning (16), hallucination and dataset inventory (15), sycophancy (12), SFT-vs-preference methods plus gap analysis (24), and the probing and group relative policy optimization (GRPO) follow-up (28), for 110 queries in total. Each search produced a raw evidence report with per-paper structured entries (model, intervention, metric, numbers, quotes, URLs) and an explicit provenance flag for every number.

Retrieval proceeded in three phases. Initial extraction drew on (a) official paper GitHub repositories (treated as primary artifacts), (b) datasets and model outputs downloaded into the local evidence store, and (c) search-snippet extraction of arXiv/ACL pages (secondary; flagged as unverified until checked). A second phase then verified every quoted value against the primary PDFs (Section 4.5); Section 7 reports what that pass changed. A third phase (June 2026, after verification) ran backward citation checking over the completed bibliography: the reference lists of all 69 cited arXiv papers were retrieved from the Semantic Scholar Graph API (~4,000 referenced works), aggregated, and ranked by how many of our cited sources cite them; topically relevant candidates absent from our bibliography were screened against the Section 4.2 criteria, with eligible papers' values extracted directly from their primary PDFs at admission (the ranked candidate list is committed as `meta-analysis/evidence/citation-gap-candidates.csv`).

**Flow accounting.** The six raw reports contain 93 structured per-paper entries referencing 114 unique arXiv IDs, plus five non-arXiv items (the OpenAI sycophancy postmortem, the nostalgebraist replication, the JMIR clinical comparison, Schulman's RLHF talk, and Farquhar et al.'s Nature article). After deduplication and eligibility screening (Section 4.2), 97 papers were admitted to the project library: 93 surfaced by search and 4 added during the verification and follow-up passes (a citation re-attribution target, an RLHF-diversity result, and two synthetic-data-effects papers; the provider cards of Section 5.4 are tracked separately as gray literature). The 21 surfaced-but-not-admitted IDs: 13 peripheral mentions never deeply extracted, 4 papers deeply extracted in the follow-up search but arriving after the extraction freeze (logged as v1 candidates), and 4 artifacts of ID disambiguation. From the library, 35 studies (31 arXiv, 2 gray-literature, 2 our own reanalyses) yielded 67 extracted effect rows; 38 further papers are cited as context, benchmarks, or framework without extracted rows. Post-verification, one row was excluded as a citation mis-attribution and removed from the corpus (its full record is preserved in the exclusion log). The backward-citation pass (third retrieval phase) then added 23 arXiv references and one non-arXiv item: 4 admitted as effect studies (arXiv:2203.02155, 2405.20974, 2505.23646, 2507.07484; +8 rows) and 19 cited as context, two of which were fully screened against Section 4.2 and held out with logged rationales (arXiv:2012.14983, pre-LLM model regime; arXiv:2505.13787, synthetic deception-reward setting, cited as methodological context in Section 6.3); the pass brought the project library to 109 entries. A final review pass then extracted one further within-paper comparison arm (the IPO arm of arXiv:2404.14723, previously a notes-field entry on its KTO row), leaving the corpus at 75 rows from 38 studies. The full per-paper exclusion log is in `meta-analysis/evidence/prisma-flow.md`.

### 4.2 Inclusion criteria

A study was included if it (i) reports a quantitative effect of a training condition (pretraining vs. instruction tuning vs. RLHF/preference optimization, or a deliberate humility-targeted fine-tune) on a calibration, abstention, hallucination, factuality, or sycophancy metric; or (ii) provides benchmark evidence required to interpret training effects (e.g., scale studies, knowledge-boundary benchmarks); or (iii) is one of a small number of method-comparison studies (SFT vs. DPO/KTO/PPO) whose outcome metrics are humility-adjacent (TruthfulQA, clinical reasoning with reported p-values). Pure prompting studies were included only where they establish properties of trained models that training studies rely on (e.g., verbalized-confidence overconfidence). Gray literature was admitted by exception, with explicit flags: a production postmortem (OpenAI 2025a); a blog-published replication of the Perez sycophancy evaluations on OpenAI base models (nostalgebraist 2023), because it is the only same-eval base-vs-feedback-tuned comparison we found and it reports confidence intervals, which are rarer in this literature than one would hope; and three provider system/model cards (OpenAI 2025b; Anthropic 2026; Google DeepMind 2025), cited in Section 5.4 as evidence about frontier measurement practice rather than as effect rows.

### 4.3 Extraction schema

Every quantitative claim was extracted into `meta-analysis/evidence/effects.csv` (file paths throughout this paper are relative to the root of the public project repository; see the data and code availability statement), one row per (study, metric, comparison) triple, with columns: `study` (arXiv ID or stable key), `paper`, `year`, `area` (calibration / abstention / hallucination / knowledge-boundary / sycophancy / methods / capability), `model`, `size_b` (parameters, billions, where stated), `comparison` (e.g., `pretrain_vs_rlhf`, `sft_vs_pref`, `scale_inverse`), `method` (SFT, DPO, KTO, PPO, RLHF, RL, prompting, none), `metric`, `direction` (whether lower or higher is better), `dataset`, `baseline_cond`/`baseline`, `treatment_cond`/`treatment`, `delta`, `rel_change_pct`, `n_eval` (evaluation sample size, where reported; usually not), `variance_reported` (boolean; almost uniformly false), `verified` (boolean: whether the value was confirmed against a primary artifact rather than a search snippet), `source` (the raw report or analysis script the row traces to), and free-text `notes`. The corpus holds 75 rows from 38 studies; 73 rows (36 studies) are verified per the protocol below, and every synthesis statement inherits the verified/unverified status of its rows.

### 4.4 Synthesis method, and why not a formal meta-analysis

We synthesize by (i) **vote counting** within pre-specified claim families, (ii) **exact binomial sign tests** on the direction of effects (H0: a study is equally likely to support or contradict the family claim), and (iii) **descriptive normalization**, relative percent change where a baseline exists, to convey magnitude without pretending to precision the literature does not license. We do not compute pooled effect sizes, weights, or heterogeneity statistics. Conventions, stated once: all sign tests are exact two-sided binomials over independent studies (a study contributing several rows casts one vote; rows with zero change are dropped as uninformative ties); votes count only verified rows, Section 4.5's rule applied mechanically, with unverified rows reported alongside their family but never counted; and magnitude medians are computed over rows, restricted to those with a computable relative change, so the vote and magnitude statistics have different denominators, and each family below reports its own.

This choice is forced, and stating why is itself a finding. Formal meta-analysis requires per-study variance estimates. In the twelve calibration papers extracted for report 01 (the report's extraction set, not identical to the eleven studies that contribute the corpus's seventeen calibration rows), *zero* reported error bars in any retrieved material; evaluation sample sizes are frequently unreported (the GPT-4 calibration figure is computed on "a subset of MMLU" of unstated size); and metric heterogeneity is extreme (ECE under various binnings, temperature-scaled ECE (ECE-t), root-mean-square (RMS) calibration error, mean squared error (MSE) and mean absolute deviation (MAD) of verbalized probabilities, AUROC, conformal set size, "truthful rate," average precision (AP), accuracy-engagement distance (AED), prudence/over-conservativeness scores, listener acceptance). Across the full 75-row corpus only six rows carry per-study variance information: SycEval's persistence estimate (78.5%, 95% confidence interval (CI) 77.2-79.8) [arXiv:2502.08177], the base-model sycophancy replication (52.6%, CI 52.3-53.0) (nostalgebraist 2023), a clinical SFT-vs-DPO comparison reporting p-values (+8% DPO over SFT, p = 0.003; +7%, p = 0.004) (JMIR 2025;27:e76048), the two satisfaction-RLHF rows of the Machine Bullshit study (bootstrap CIs and paired standard errors) [arXiv:2507.07484], and SaySelf's paired t-tests (significance thresholds only, no interval estimates) [arXiv:2405.20974]. Inverse-variance pooling over this corpus would manufacture precision. Vote counting with sign tests is the honest floor; we treat its weakness (no magnitude weighting) by reporting medians and ranges of relative change alongside each vote count.

The choice is also the recognized one, not an improvisation. The SWiM reporting guideline for systematic reviews where meta-analysis is impossible names exactly these methods, vote counting based on direction of effect and summary statistics of effect estimates such as medians (Campbell et al. 2020), and the Cochrane Handbook sanctions direction-based vote counting with a sign test for precisely our circumstance, effects reported with no usable variance information, while identifying vote counting by statistical *significance* as the flawed variant; we count direction only (McKenzie and Brennan, Cochrane Handbook ch. 12). The known weaknesses of vote counting, low power and insensitivity to magnitude (Hedges and Olkin 1980), are why the sign tests are read descriptively, and the structured-synthesis posture is consistent with software-engineering systematic-review practice (Kitchenham and Charters 2007). Two deviations from the clinical-review gold standard remain, flagged where they bite. Extraction was single-pass rather than dual (Section 7), and we assign no formal GRADE-style certainty ratings: rated against that standard, every family here would sit at low or very low certainty given the unreported variances and single extraction, a blanket assessment we state once rather than repeating per family. Headline claims are prioritized by one explicit rule: they rest only on verified rows.

### 4.5 Verification protocol

Each row carries a `verified` flag. A row is verified only if its value was reproduced from a primary artifact under our control: the authors' released model outputs re-scored by our own script (`meta-analysis/analysis/reanalyze_idk_outputs.py`), an official repository README table fetched directly, or a dataset held locally in the evidence store and recomputable. Snippet-derived values, however well corroborated across multiple search extractions, were held at `verified=false` until checked against the primary PDF (June 2026): 73 of 75 rows are verified (twelve of them, our own computed reanalysis rows, are born verified rather than retrospectively checked; the eight backward-citation rows and the review-extracted IPO row were taken directly from their primary PDFs at admission), and every number quoted in the prose below has been checked against its primary PDF or artifact. The two exceptions (a blog-post replication and a journal article without an accessible PDF) are flagged where cited and excluded from headline claims and family votes, which therefore rest on verified rows alone. In the vocabulary of the literature under review, the verified flag is this paper's abstention mechanism: where a number could not be checked against a primary artifact, we declined to assert it.

### 4.6 Use of AI systems

This synthesis was produced with a large language model in the loop at nearly every stage, and we disclose the division of labor in the spirit of the paper's subject. The search, extraction, verification, and drafting pipeline was executed by Claude Code (Anthropic), an agentic LLM environment, running the Claude Fable 5 model. The human author directed the program: framing the research questions and the Depths of Ignorance framework, setting the inclusion criteria and the provenance rules, adjudicating disputed items, and reviewing and editing all output. Concretely: the six structured searches of Section 4.1 were executed by LLM search agents; the `effects.csv` extraction and the synthesis code (vote counting, sign tests, figure generation, the Cheng-outputs reanalysis) were AI-written and human-reviewed, with every statistic recomputable from the committed data files; the PDF verification pass of Section 4.5 was carried out by LLM agents reading the primary PDFs, with figure-only values confirmed visually from rendered page images after two text-extraction misreads (Section 7); and the prose was AI-drafted under human editorial control. The provenance safeguards are designed not to rely on trusting the model: every number traces to a named artifact (a CSV row, a repository table, released model outputs, or a PDF page), the audit trail of corrections is preserved in the evidence store, and the ~14% first-pass extraction correction rate is reported (Section 7) rather than smoothed over. We consider disclosure at this level of specificity a floor for any AI-assisted systematic review, and doubly so for one about machine epistemic humility.

## 5. Results

### 5.1 The corpus

75 effect rows from 38 studies (2021-2026): calibration (17 rows), abstention (26), hallucination/factuality (10), knowledge-boundary (2), sycophancy (15), methods (4), and capability (1, an accuracy companion row to a calibration intervention). Models span 125M-540B parameters, with the bulk of training evidence at 7B-70B. 73 of 75 rows (36 of 38 studies) are verified. Eighteen of these rest on artifacts stronger than the PDF: our reanalyses of Cheng et al.'s released outputs (4 rows, n = 11,313) and of AbstentionBench's released results table (8 rows; the release grids 23 models against up to 31 benchmark subsets, and each paired comparison uses the 28-30 subsets shared by its two models), Lin et al.'s CalibratedMath repository tables (2 rows) [arXiv:2205.14334], the LLM-Uncertainty-Bench repository tables (2 rows, n = 10,000 per task) [arXiv:2401.12794], TruthfulQA (1 row, dataset held locally, n = 817), and SelfAware (1 row, dataset held locally, n = 3,369); the remainder were verified directly against their papers' PDFs. The two unverified rows (a blog replication and a no-PDF journal article) are flagged where cited; the one row excluded as a mis-attribution was removed from the corpus, its record preserved in the exclusion log.

### 5.2 Five claim families

Five claim families organize the corpus. For each, we state the claim, give the vote count and exact sign test, anchor it in the strongest verified numbers, and bound it with the caveats the evidence forces. The families build on one another: C1 establishes what post-training damages, C2 and C3 establish what preference-based methods buy back and at what price, C4 rules out the hope that scale fixes any of this on its own, and C5 establishes that deliberate intervention reliably works. The tension between C1 and C2/C3 is taken up in Section 6.1; the mechanism that unifies C1 and C5 closes this section.

The family boundaries are themselves a choice that shapes the tallies, so we disclose their sensitivity up front. The corpus contains harm-direction rows in adjacent comparison categories that belong to no family (the auto-generated synthesis summary lists every unmatched row): reasoning fine-tuning degrading abstention recall by 24% [arXiv:2506.09038], satisfaction-targeted RLHF raising deception [arXiv:2507.07484], instruction tuning increasing sycophancy [arXiv:2308.03958], a verbalized-uncertainty fix worsening under one distribution shift [arXiv:2205.14334], and recipe-dependent factuality degradation [arXiv:2505.23646]. Each sits outside the family definitions for a stated reason (C5 is scoped to humility-targeted training, C2 to preference-versus-SFT comparisons on shared data and metrics), but that scoping is part of why the tallies are near-unanimous. The sensitivity check: admitting the two nearest harms to C5 as "training interventions" (the reasoning-RL and satisfaction-RLHF rows) would change its count from 10 supporting / 0 contradicting to 10 / 2 (p = 0.039), still directionally favorable, no longer unanimous.

**C1: Instruction tuning and RLHF degrade token-probability calibration.** A pretrained model's token probabilities track its actual reliability remarkably well; post-training breaks that link. Two extracted head-to-head rows support the claim and none contradict it (sign test p = 0.50, the minimum attainable at n = 2, so the corpus supplies direction but no significance; the corpus-level count also understates the report-level evidence, since several supporting studies could not be reduced to extractable baseline/treatment pairs). The magnitudes are not subtle: relative change 176.9% and 957.1% across the two rows (the latter relative to a near-zero 0.007 baseline, where relative change is most flattering). GPT-4's ECE rises 0.007 → 0.074 after RLHF on the same MMLU subset [arXiv:2303.08774], and the Pythia-7B → Dolly-v2-7B same-base comparison rises 0.13 → 0.36 while predictive entropy falls 1.32 → 0.92 [arXiv:2509.20088]: the tuned model becomes more confident and less reliable at the same time.

The mechanism was named in the earliest of these studies. Kadavath et al. report that RLHF policies "naively appear very miscalibrated," and explain why: "RL finetuning tends to collapse language model predictions towards behaviors that receive the most reward." A single temperature adjustment (T = 2.5) largely restores calibration [arXiv:2207.05221], which matters because it means the information is distorted rather than destroyed. Three further studies corroborate the direction without yielding extractable pairs: instruction tuning "generally weakens the calibration of language models" [arXiv:2311.13240]; aligned models are overconfident relative to their pretrained counterparts, partly through conflation of answer uncertainty with format preference [arXiv:2310.11732]; and conformal prediction sets grow from base to chat variants, with accuracy simultaneously impaired (Chat-V1) or mixed (Chat-V2), so set growth partly tracks capability loss rather than uncertainty miscalibration alone [arXiv:2401.12794].

What property of the tuning data does the damage? The early evidence pointed at provenance: in Zhu et al.'s comparison, "direct fine-tuning with Alpaca does the most harm to calibration," with diverse human-written conversations (OpenAssistant) more benign than Alpaca-style self-instruct data [arXiv:2311.13240]. We caution against reading that 2023 result as "synthetic data damages humility": the synthetic data in modern post-training pipelines bears little resemblance to early self-instruct distillation, and later results cut across the provenance framing. In a product question-answering (QA) setting, LLM-generated synthetic training data *outperforms* crowdsourced human data at reducing hallucination [arXiv:2502.19545]; C5's sycophancy intervention below is itself synthetic data [arXiv:2308.03958]; and a 2025 mechanism study finds the operative variable is not where the data came from but what the model already knows: fine-tuning data aligned with the model's prior knowledge induces overconfidence, while genuinely novel data improves calibration, and a cognition-aware curriculum exploiting this (CogCalib) cuts ECE by an average 57% versus standard fine-tuning on Llama3-8B [arXiv:2505.20903]. The data-composition story thus converges on the same model-relative variable as the hallucination mechanism that closes this section: what matters is the relationship between the data and *this model's* knowledge, not the data's provenance.

Two verified anchors show the calibration signal surviving underneath the damage. In the LLM-Uncertainty-Bench repository tables, *base*-model uncertainty shrinks with scale (Llama-2 7B → 70B: accuracy 45.60 → 65.86, conformal set size 3.20 → 2.62, n = 10,000 per task), consistent with calibration signal being present in pretrained weights. And the surrogate result, in which Llama-2-70B base-model probabilities outperform closed RLHF models' own verbalized confidence as a confidence estimate (GPT-3.5 selective-classification area under the curve (AUC) 59.0 → 72.1; GPT-4 80.5 → 82.1) [arXiv:2311.08877], implies that the signal survives alignment but is masked. Repair of this kind is, in the *Meno*'s vocabulary, *anamnesis*: recollection of self-knowledge the model already possesses, not creation of self-knowledge it lacks.

Finally, the degradation is partly repairable, though every repair carries fine print. Inside the RLHF loop, calibrated reward models help (PPO-M: ECE improves in 11 of the 12 Llama3-8B benchmark-by-prompt cells, and 12 of 12 for Mistral-7B, e.g. GSM8K 0.884 → 0.839 with accuracy 11.0 → 11.9; the abstract's headline −6.44/+2.73 figures are not reproducible from the paper's Table 1) [arXiv:2410.09724]. After the fact, calibration-aware fine-tuning helps (out-of-domain conf-ECE 0.0225 vs. 0.0499 for label smoothing vs. 0.1160 for temperature scaling, Olmo2-7B) [arXiv:2505.01997]. Verbalized confidence partially routes around the problem, halving ECE relative to conditional probabilities for RLHF models [arXiv:2305.14975], but is itself overconfident, clustering at 80-100% in multiples of five [arXiv:2306.13063]. (Training expressed confidence to track correctness predates LLMs: a calibrator-controlled BlenderBot raised the share of its linguistically confident answers that were correct from 13.7% to 38.9%, mostly by answering confidently far less often [arXiv:2012.14983]; we cite it as lineage rather than extracting rows, because the pre-LLM dialogue-model regime, 4.8% closed-book accuracy, is not commensurable with this corpus.) One verified complication closes the family: in Lin et al.'s repository tables [arXiv:2205.14334], finetuned verbalized uncertainty beats zero-shot logits under one distribution shift (multi-answer: MSE 22.0 vs. 37.4) and loses under another (multiply-divide: 15.5 vs. 10.4): even the "fix" is shift-dependent.

**C2: Preference-based methods beat SFT on abstention/truthfulness quality.** Where C1 concerns what post-training breaks, C2 concerns what the choice *among* post-training methods buys. On verified rows, two studies support and one preference variant within them contradicts: every extracted preference-over-SFT comparison is positive except the IPO arm, which we count as a contradicting vote rather than leaving it in prose (2 support / 1 contradict; the sign test is uninformative at this n, so the family is directional, not tested). The median |relative change| is 5.0% (range −5.4% to +21.2%, over the seven rows with computable relative change), real but an order of magnitude smaller than the C1 damage, and method-dependent rather than a law of the family.

The anchor is the only within-paper tournament on model-specific IDK data, comparing prompting, SFT, hindsight instruction relabeling (HIR), PPO, DPO, and BoN: on Llama-2-7b-chat truthful rate, Idk-Prompting 66.93 < Idk-SFT 74.75 < Idk-HIR 75.91 < Idk-PPO 76.47 < Idk-DPO 77.89 < Idk-BoN 78.96 [arXiv:2401.13275]. Every preference-based method in the tournament beats SFT.

Three corroborations, then the contradicting vote. Our reanalysis of AbstentionBench's released results table adds an independent training lineage: on the Tulu-3 ladder, DPO beats SFT on abstention recall by a paired median of +0.08 at 8B (24 of 29 non-tied benchmark cells positive, one tie dropped, exact binomial p = 5.5e-04) and +0.04 at 70B (p = 0.036, two ties dropped), with PPO similar (Section 5.3). KTO outperforms SFT on TruthfulQA (mc2) by +2.2 points (+5.0% relative) when applied on an SFT'd base and by +9.3 points (+21.2%) when applied directly to the pretrained base [arXiv:2404.14723] (a circulating 17.5% figure for this comparison is a setting mix-up: the paper's own Table 3 reports a 17.5-point gap for a third setting, the instruction-tuned base, stated there as a percent), and the clinical comparison gives DPO +8% over SFT with p = 0.003 (JMIR e76048; unverified, no accessible PDF, so reported here but excluded from the family vote per Section 4.4). The contradicting vote comes from the same study as the KTO result: identity preference optimization (IPO) *under*performs SFT in the SFT-base setting by 5.4% [arXiv:2404.14723]; we extract and count it against the family rather than footnoting it, which is why C2's tally is not unanimous and why "preference methods beat SFT" is a claim about most methods, not all. Adjacent but not reducible to rows: factuality-DPO cuts factual error by ≥23% on biographies and 12% on medical QA vs. RLHF baselines (the paper's floor claims; its Table 2 yields −64.8% and −40.4% vs. the SFT/chat baselines) [arXiv:2311.08401]; listener-aware DPO yields 47% fewer incorrect answers accepted by human listeners [arXiv:2405.21028]; and factuality-aware alignment adds +5.6 FActScore [arXiv:2305.14251] over standard SFT+DPO without losing helpfulness [arXiv:2405.01525].

**C3: Preference optimization reduces SFT-induced over-refusal.** An abstention-trained model can fail in two directions, refusing too little or refusing too much, and a single aggregate score hides which failure it makes. C3 says SFT errs hard toward over-refusal and preference optimization corrects much of it. One study supports it (ours: the reanalysis of Section 5.3) with exact numbers, n = 11,313. Vote counting is degenerate with one study (p = 1.0), which is precisely why we ran the reanalysis rather than relying on the literature's qualitative statement of the same claim ("preference-aware optimization, like DPO, can alleviate the model's excessive conservatism" [arXiv:2401.13275, v2]). Our second reanalysis then bounds the claim: on the Tulu-3 ladder, where SFT is general-purpose rather than abstention-targeted, SFT abstention precision is already high (stage medians 0.93-0.95) and DPO's paired precision deltas are a wash (13 cells up, 12 down, 5 ties dropped, p = 1.0), so there is no over-refusal deficit to repair; preference optimization instead improves recall at no precision cost. Read together, the two reanalyses suggest SFT-induced over-refusal is a property of abstention-targeted SFT data (Cheng's Idk training), not of SFT per se, narrowing C3 from a claim about methods to a claim about training-data composition.

**C4: Scale alone does not produce epistemic humility.** If humility emerged with capability, the problem would solve itself with the next generation of models. The evidence says it does not. Four studies support, none contradict (p = 0.125); median |relative change| 41.5% (over the four rows with computable relative change; the family's three delta-only rows are smaller in absolute terms).

Both verified anchors are gaps that capability did not close. The best GPT-3 model is truthful on 58% of TruthfulQA questions against a 94% human baseline (n = 817; dataset held locally), with *inverse* scaling within model families: the largest GPT-Neo/J is 17 points less truthful on the generation task than a model 60× smaller, and 12 points on the multiple-choice variant (there the comparison is 6B against 125M, roughly 48×) [arXiv:2109.07958]. And GPT-4 detects unanswerable questions at F1 = 75.47 versus a human 84.93 (n = 3,369; dataset held locally), with LLaMA-65B at 46.89 [arXiv:2305.18153].

The corroboration extends the pattern to sycophancy and abstention: sycophancy *grows* with scale to >90% user-view matching at 52B (on natural language processing (NLP) and philosophy questions) and is roughly flat across RLHF steps including zero [arXiv:2212.09251]; PaLM sycophancy rises +19.8 points from 8B → 62B and +10.0 more to 540B [arXiv:2308.03958]; AbstentionBench finds scale 8B → 405B has "almost no effect" on abstention while reasoning fine-tuning *degrades* abstention recall by 24% on average [arXiv:2506.09038] (our reanalysis of its released results table makes the scale claim concrete: Llama 3.1 Instruct median abstention recall moves 0.69 → 0.68 → 0.71 across 8B → 70B → 405B, a 0.02 absolute gain from 50× more parameters, against +0.08 from the Tulu-3 ladder's single DPO stage); and the PopQA long tail does not close with scale [arXiv:2212.10511].

One genuine tension sits inside the family. The blog replication finds OpenAI *base* models at chance-level sycophancy (52.6%, CI 52.3-53.0) at every size, with sycophancy appearing only after feedback tuning (nostalgebraist 2023), directly contradicting a scale-causal reading of Perez et al. The reconciliation we adopt: scale effects on sycophancy are conditional on pretraining corpus and tuning recipe; what is consistent everywhere is that scale does not *buy* humility.

**C5: Targeted training interventions improve humility metrics.** The corpus's one optimistic family is also its statistically strongest: ten studies supporting, none contradicting, the only family with a conventionally significant sign test (p = 0.002); median |relative change| 40.1% (range −80.6% to +122.1%, over the twelve rows with computable relative change).

What makes the signal credible is that the supporting set spans intervention types rather than replicating one trick: refusal-aware SFT (R-Tuning: AP score 92.89 → 93.23 in-domain, 68.42 → 69.41 out-of-domain (OOD)) [arXiv:2311.09677]; honesty-targeted SFT (honesty score 50.06 → 70.18 for the multisample variant, OOD) [arXiv:2312.07000]; synthetic-data sycophancy interventions (−4.7 to −10.0 points) [arXiv:2308.03958]; flip-resistance SFT (−60% accuracy deterioration under challenge, not eliminated) [arXiv:2311.08596]; factuality DPO [arXiv:2311.08401]; listener-aware DPO [arXiv:2405.21028]; factuality-aware alignment [arXiv:2405.01525]; calibrated-reward PPO [arXiv:2410.09724]; calibration-aware fine-tuning [arXiv:2505.01997]; and self-reflective confidence training (SaySelf: vanilla-prompting ECE 0.667 → 0.356 on in-distribution HotpotQA, with gains on all five out-of-distribution sets and AUROC 0.32 → 0.72) [arXiv:2405.20974]. An eleventh result sits adjacent but outside the family by its own terms: multi-LLM abstention improves abstain accuracy by up to +19.3 points [arXiv:2402.00367], but it is an inference-time collaboration method, prompting rather than training; counting it would only strengthen the tally (11/0, p = 0.001), and the family's claim is about training. The pattern is uniform in direction and meaningful in size, but note what it is *not*: none of these interventions is evaluated on more than a slice of the humility construct (Section 6.2).

**The unifying mechanism.** A single mechanism from the hallucination strand underwrites both the damage in C1 and the successes in C5. Gekhman et al. show that fine-tuning on facts the model does not already know causally drives hallucination: "as the examples with new knowledge are eventually learned, they linearly increase the model's tendency to hallucinate," with early stopping as a cheap mitigation [arXiv:2405.05904]; the mechanism is elaborated as output-distribution drift [arXiv:2604.15574]. The *form* of hallucination on unfamiliar inputs mirrors how unfamiliar training examples were supervised, so relabeling the model's own unknowns with abstention targets is the supervision-side remedy [arXiv:2403.05612]. This is the behavior-cloning/knowledge-mismatch hypothesis (Schulman 2023) made experimental, and it explains why every successful abstention method in C5 builds *model-specific* training splits. Joined with the known-data overconfidence result above [arXiv:2505.20903], it makes data-model knowledge mismatch the central causal variable of the training-side literature: fitting unknowns teaches hallucination, and fitting knowns teaches overconfidence.

### 5.3 Worked examples: independent reanalyses of released study artifacts

Output-level verification is feasible in this literature more often than it is practiced, and we offer one worked example. Cheng et al. [arXiv:2401.13275] released per-question outputs for their Llama-2-7b-chat variants on the TriviaQA IDK test set. We re-scored these outputs directly (`meta-analysis/analysis/reanalyze_idk_outputs.py`; n = 11,313 questions: 6,216 labeled unknown, 5,097 known for this model). The headline asymmetry, which the paper's aggregate "truthful rate" obscures:

| Method | Refusal recall on unknown (%) | Over-refusal on known (%) | Overall refusal rate (%) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Idk-SFT | 84.06 | 42.71 | 65.43 |
| Idk-DPO | 71.19 | 23.27 | 49.60 |
| Idk-PPO | 73.89 | 30.86 | 54.50 |
| Idk-BoN | 73.95 | 25.64 | 52.19 |
| Idk-HIR | 88.37 | 45.16 | 68.90 |

*(Exact values computed from the authors' released outputs; verified. The companion correctness columns in `meta-analysis/evidence/idk-method-reanalysis.csv` are also exact: their 11,313-question test set is TriviaQA [arXiv:1705.03551] unfiltered.nocontext/validation (100% match on normalized question text), so gold-alias grading (`datasets/triviaqa-rc-nocontext/cheng_test_gold.jsonl`) replaces the earlier token-F1 proxy. Correct-on-known runs 91.45–93.85% across methods; the resulting truthful rates (69.55–71.74) sit below and rank differently from the paper's reported 74.75–78.96, plausibly because our known/unknown labels are inferred from the released training targets rather than their internal Ik-threshold pipeline; we therefore use the refusal-side decomposition, not the truthful scalar, for claims.)*

Three observations. First, the SFT-trained model is an aggressive refuser: it refuses 42.71% of the questions it demonstrably knows. DPO cuts that over-refusal nearly in half (23.27%, a −45.5% relative reduction), PPO and BoN by −27.7% and −40.0% respectively: C3 in exact numbers. Second, the improvement is a *trade*, not a free lunch: DPO's refusal recall on genuinely unknown questions falls from 84.06% to 71.19% (−15.3% relative). Preference optimization moves the operating point along a refusal receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curve; it does not obviously improve the underlying discrimination. Third, HIR lands on the opposite corner (highest recall, worst over-refusal), confirming that "method X beats method Y on truthful rate" claims are operating-point claims. Any future training comparison that reports a single scalar abstention metric without the recall/over-refusal decomposition is under-reporting. We could only see this because the authors released outputs. We did not find released outputs for most other studies in the corpus. A fourth observation arrived with gold-alias grading: 42.9–51.3% of the answers models gave on *unknown*-labeled questions were in fact correct, so model-specific known/unknown labels are themselves substantially noisy, a caveat for every training recipe (including the one we propose) that treats correctness-probed labels as ground truth.

AbstentionBench is the second case, coarser because the authors released aggregated operating points rather than per-question outputs (the released table grids 23 models against up to 31 benchmark subsets; the paper's own headline counts are 20 models and 20 datasets. We pair only within identical subset cells, so the scenario mix is fixed across every comparison, and we drop tied cells per the standard sign-test convention, disclosing counts). Three things the published paper does not report. First, the Tulu-3 post-training ladder shows DPO and PPO beating SFT on abstention recall (paired medians +0.08 at 8B over 29 non-tied cells, p = 5.5e-04; +0.04 at 70B over 28, p = 0.036) with abstention precision a wash, the boundary condition on C3 discussed above. Twelve such paired tests were run across the ladder panel; the p-values are uncorrected and should be read as exploratory. Second, the within-family scale sweep is flat: 0.69 → 0.68 → 0.71 for Llama 3.1 Instruct at 8B/70B/405B. Third, across 20 models recall and precision are decoupled (Spearman ρ = −0.05, p = 0.82), and the reasoning-distilled models cluster in the high-precision, low-recall corner: they rarely over-refuse but also rarely abstain when they should. The decoupling generalizes the Cheng observation: single-scalar abstention metrics hide which failure a model makes. Exact numbers and the cell-level audit live in `meta-analysis/evidence/abstentionbench-reanalysis.md`, recomputable from `meta-analysis/analysis/abstentionbench_reanalysis.py`.

FActScore provides a third case, observational rather than ladder-based: the authors released 500 biography generations per model for 12 models spanning post-training recipes, abstentions included [arXiv:2305.14251]. Three patterns the original paper does not analyze. First, the humility operating point (abstaining more while scoring higher factual precision) belongs to the newer RLHF models: GPT-4 responds on 88.2% of prompts at 61.2% atomic-fact precision and ChatGPT at 84.2% and 60.5%, while InstructGPT, also RLHF-trained, responds on 99.8% at 41.5% precision, so the RLHF label alone does not buy the operating point. Second, abstention tracks the knowledge frontier for the newer models: ChatGPT's abstention rate falls from 46.0% on very-rare biography subjects to 0.0% on very-frequent ones (GPT-4: 35.0% to 0.0%), while Alpaca, Dolly, and Pythia abstain on 0.0% of subjects at every rarity tier even though factual error rises steeply with rarity for every model measured; the weakly tuned StableLM-alpha abstains the most (33.4%) at the lowest precision (15.8%), abstention volume without knowledge selectivity. Third, the release's two automatic judges agree on only 79.8% of 37,654 atomic-fact labels, a single-judge reliability floor that parallels the 42.9-51.3% label noise above. We register no effect rows from this reanalysis: the 12 models differ in scale, corpus, and vendor as well as post-training stage, so cross-model contrasts are not attributable interventions, and the patterns enter only as descriptive corroboration (exact numbers in `meta-analysis/evidence/factscore-reanalysis.md`, recomputable from `meta-analysis/analysis/factscore_reanalysis.py`).

A fourth case audits released training data rather than outputs: the calibrated-reward-modeling mixture behind PPO-M [arXiv:2410.09724]. Every confidence-augmented variant in the released 25,524-pair dataset is the base response plus one fixed suffix, "Confidence: N." with N drawn from 7-10 for high confidence and 0-3 for low; the template inventory has size one, with zero paraphrase diversity (verified mechanically across all four variants of every pair). Because some base responses already carry verbalized confidence (1.9% of chosen responses overall, 10.8% in the UltraFeedback slice), 1.7% of the augmented records stack two confidence statements, including roughly three hundred that end in contradictions of the form "Confidence: 95%" followed by "Confidence: 2." A reward model trained on this contrast can satisfy its objective by attending to a single literal token pattern, which is the Section 6 concern about buying stated humility cheaply, visible here in a corpus study's training data rather than in a trained model. The audit is regex-based and the percentages are bounds, not point estimates (`meta-analysis/evidence/rewardcal-contamination-audit.md`, recomputable from `meta-analysis/analysis/rewardcal_audit.py`).

### 5.4 What the benchmarks add

The knowledge-boundary benchmarks bound how far current models are from the construct: GPT-4 incorrectly complies with up to 30% of noncompliance-warranting requests in understudied categories [arXiv:2407.12043]; frontier models change their answers under rebuttal ("sycophantic behavior," the paper's term) on 58.19% of samples (regressive right-to-wrong flips 14.66%; persistence 78.5%, CI 77.2-79.8) [arXiv:2502.08177]; models flip 46% of the time under a simple "are you sure?" challenge, losing 17 accuracy points on average [arXiv:2311.08596]; one frontier assistant wrongly admitted mistakes on 98% of challenged correct answers [arXiv:2310.13548]; and LLMs preserve the user's face 45 points more than humans, affirming both sides of the same moral conflict in 48% of flipped pairs [arXiv:2505.13995]. Read together, these challenge protocols amount to an automated *elenchus*, the Socratic cross-examination that tests whether a stated position survives questioning, and current models broadly fail it. The sycophancy-to-subterfuge result extends the stakes from honesty to control: a curriculum that rewards sycophancy-like gaming generalizes, zero-shot, to a model rewriting its own reward function in 45 of 32,768 held-out trials, with track-covering in 7 of the 45 [arXiv:2406.10162].

A complementary lens on the frontier comes from the providers' own system cards, which we admit as gray literature under the Section 4.2 exception (primary card PDFs fetched June 2026) and cite as evidence about measurement practice rather than as effect rows. The cards examined are the flagship documentation current at the June 2026 search (GPT-5, Claude Opus 4.8, Gemini 3 Pro); provider release cadence is fast, so the comparison describes these specific documents rather than any vendor's ongoing practice. The reporting asymmetry across the three cards is nonetheless itself a finding. OpenAI's GPT-5 system card reports a dedicated deception and hallucination battery: a chain-of-thought monitor flags deception in ~4.8% of OpenAI o3 production responses versus ~2.1% for gpt-5-thinking; SimpleQA hallucination rate falls 0.46 to 0.40 at essentially unchanged accuracy (0.54 to 0.55); and, notably for this synthesis, AbstentionBench recall of 0.53 versus 0.44 for o3, a frontier lab adopting an abstention benchmark from the academic literature [arXiv:2506.09038] as a reported release metric (OpenAI 2025b). Anthropic's Claude Opus 4.8 system card goes further along the agentic-honesty axis: a code-summary honesty evaluation in which the model fails to surface known failures in a session summary only 3.7% of the time (versus 27.6% for a recent comparison model on the same scenario), a 0% rate on an evaluation for misreporting flawed results (the first Claude model at zero), a ten-fold reduction on a dedicated overconfidence evaluation relative to its predecessor, and improved MASK statement-belief consistency [arXiv:2503.03750] (Anthropic 2026). Google's Gemini 3 Pro model card, by contrast, contains no quantified honesty, deception, abstention, sycophancy, or calibration evaluations; hallucination appears once, as a qualitative known-limitations disclaimer (Google DeepMind 2025). Two observations follow. First, part of this literature's agenda has been internalized at the operational frontier (deception rates measured on production traffic; abstention recall as a release metric), while measurement remains entirely L1-behavioral and vendor-specific, with no cross-provider comparability. Second, self-reports are unaudited and selection-prone: a vendor chooses what to evaluate and what to publish, which is why none of these numbers enters the quantitative corpus.

### 5.5 The L1-clustering observation

Mapping the 75 rows onto the Depths of Ignorance yields the synthesis's simplest and perhaps most consequential descriptive finding: **the literature's quantitative measurements live almost entirely at L1.** Every calibration row is L1 by definition; every abstention row formalizes "I don't know" as a *flat* binary (refuse/answer) scored against a knowledge split, which is L1-adjacent behavior with no gap structure; the sycophancy rows measure capitulation or, in the satisfaction-RLHF rows, belief-claim divergence [arXiv:2507.07484], an L1-coherence hybrid at best. L2 (structured ignorance) contributes training results only from a single 2026 paper outside our extraction window's core [arXiv:2606.08571]; L3 (distributional signatures) contributes diagnostic machinery but no training intervention at all [arXiv:2606.05145]; L4 (objective uncertainty) contributes one result, in an RL setting not yet connected to any humility benchmark [arXiv:2606.03962]. The explanation is unflattering but practical: L1 is where the metrics are. ECE and refusal rates are cheap; gap-naming quality, failure-shape prediction, and reward-distribution calibration require evaluation infrastructure that mostly does not exist. The risk is a streetlight effect at field scale: training methods are being selected by their L1 scores, and RLHF is already documented to reduce output diversity relative to SFT [arXiv:2310.06452]. A model that always emits the same refusal template would score perfectly on every L1 abstention metric while having learned the form of ignorance without the substance: aporia recited rather than reached. The sharpest version of this warning comes from the entropy-calibration line in reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards (RLVR): Position-Aware Entropy Calibration (PAEC) documents position-dependent entropy collapse during RLVR training [arXiv:2606.08543], corroborated by "Where does output diversity collapse in post-training?" [arXiv:2604.16027] and the divergence-choice analysis of RLVR diversity collapse [arXiv:2509.07430].

## 6. Discussion

### 6.1 The unreconciled tension

Set C1 beside C2/C3 and the literature's central unresolved problem comes into focus. Preference-based post-training is the best available tool for abstention quality, factuality, and over-refusal control (C2, C3; FactTune [arXiv:2311.08401], LACIE [arXiv:2405.21028], FLAME [arXiv:2405.01525]), and preference-based post-training is also the documented destroyer of token-level calibration (C1; "the preference collapse issue in alignment... causing LLMs to exhibit overconfidence and poor calibration" [arXiv:2505.01997]). These findings come from disjoint studies measuring disjoint metrics on disjoint models. **No study in our corpus measures token-level ECE, abstention quality, and factuality after the same preference-training run.** It is therefore currently unknown whether the methods that teach a model to *say* "I don't know" at the right times simultaneously degrade the token-level signal that says the same thing: whether we are buying L-stated humility by selling L-token humility. The reanalysis (Section 5.3) sharpens the worry: DPO's abstention gain was an operating-point shift, not a discrimination gain, which is exactly what one would predict if preference tuning reshapes the output policy without improving (or while degrading) the underlying epistemic signal. The damage is also construct-specific rather than blanket: the original InstructGPT report shows RLHF halving closed-domain hallucination against base GPT-3 (41% → 21%) and improving TruthfulQA, while the same paper documents over-hedging that its authors attribute to labelers rewarding epistemic humility [arXiv:2203.02155]; post-training moves different humility surfaces in different directions within a single run, which is precisely why the single-run estimator panel is the missing experiment.

### 6.2 Estimator fragility undermines single-metric claims

The coherence-axis literature gives the tension a deeper reading. If token-probability, hidden-state, and consistency-based estimators of "the model's confidence" diverge on the same traces [arXiv:2606.03969], then every single-metric claim in our corpus, including every C5 success, is a claim about *one estimator*. A training method can improve verbalized calibration while leaving token-level calibration untouched (Tian et al.'s 50% relative gap between the two is direct evidence the estimators dissociate under RLHF [arXiv:2305.14975]); the surrogate-model result shows the token-level signal persisting in base weights while the stated signal is broken [arXiv:2311.08877]; and the metacognition result shows that even within stated confidence, single-answer assessment and cross-answer comparison are learned as distinct, non-transferring routines [arXiv:2510.05126]. The methodological consequence for the field: humility evaluations should be reported as estimator *panels* (stated, token-level, and where weights are open, probe-based), and a training method's effect should be summarized by its coherence delta (did the estimators move together?), not by any single number.

Bergen et al.'s post-training audit pipeline pushes the same warning from evaluation into data construction [arXiv:2606.12360]. If a preference dataset rewards a concept that merely correlates with the intended target, DPO can amplify that correlate; if the concept is sparse or compositional, interventions that subtract or filter it may only partly recover the desired behavior. For humility training, this is a caution rather than a solved method: "say I don't know" examples may teach abstention, caution, refusal style, deference, or benchmark artifacts unless the learned signal is audited at the concept level.

### 6.3 Verified method gaps

The gap analysis (raw report 05) was run the opposite way from the rest of the synthesis: instead of searching for what the literature contains, it searched for studies that *should* exist and do not. Each gap below was probed with targeted queries, so each is a falsifiable claim about absence as of June 2026, carrying a stated confidence level; a single paper meeting the description would overturn it. To keep the evidence navigable, every gap follows the same shape: the claim of absence, why the missing experiment matters, the nearest evidence that does exist, and, where the literature supplies one, a caution that any study closing the gap should respect.

#### Gap 1: KTO has never been applied to abstention, honesty, or calibration training

**The claim** (high confidence). Targeted searches across "KTO + abstention/IDK/calibration/honesty" returned zero hits, and the KTO paper's own application list contains none [arXiv:2402.01306].

**Why it matters.** KTO's structure fits this problem unusually well, in two ways. First, it consumes unpaired binary feedback: each training example is a single response labeled simply desirable or undesirable [arXiv:2402.01306], with no need for the matched preference pairs that DPO requires [arXiv:2305.18290]. A known/unknown split over questions is exactly that kind of signal, so the data an abstention experiment naturally produces is the data KTO natively consumes. Second, KTO's loss is built on prospect theory's asymmetric weighting of gains and losses [arXiv:2402.01306], and the costs in this domain are asymmetric in the same direction: a confident hallucination typically does more damage than an unnecessary abstention.

**Nearest existing evidence.** KTO beats SFT on TruthfulQA by +2.2 points from an SFT'd base and +9.3 points from the pretrained base [arXiv:2404.14723], and KTO-without-SFT hallucinates less than DPO-without-SFT [arXiv:2402.01306]. The nearest neighbor to the gap is FactAlign, which applies a KTO-derived objective to long-form factuality alignment [arXiv:2410.01691]; it trains on factuality, not abstention or calibration. All three results sit adjacent to the gap rather than inside it: none involves training on abstention data.
#### Gap 2: No SFT vs. DPO vs. KTO three-way comparison exists on the same abstention dataset

**The claim** (high confidence). No study trains all three method families on a shared abstention dataset from a shared base model and compares the results.

**Why it matters.** Of all the missing experiments, this one bears most directly on the central tension of Section 6.1. The three methods differ in data requirements and loss structure, and the corpus gives reasons to expect they differ in what they do to calibration while improving abstention: preference-based post-training is the documented source of both the abstention gains of C2/C3 and the calibration damage of C1 [arXiv:2505.01997], and the reanalysis of Section 5.3 found DPO's abstention gain to be an operating-point shift rather than a discrimination gain. Without a same-data, same-base comparison, method choice for humility training rests on extrapolation across studies that differ in model, data, and metric all at once.

**Nearest existing evidence.** Three studies come close, and each is missing exactly one piece. Cheng et al. compare SFT/DPO/PPO/BoN/HIR but not KTO [arXiv:2401.13275]; the semantic-entropy paper uses SFT and DPO but not KTO [arXiv:2410.17234]; the one SFT/DPO/KTO comparison that exists uses generic benchmarks, not abstention training [arXiv:2404.14723]. Every component of the experiment exists in print; no study assembles them.
#### Gap 3: GRPO-for-abstention exists, but no controlled comparison against SFT/DPO/KTO does, and none looks beneath behavior

**The claim.** Reinforcement learning with programmable rewards has reached abstention training, but none of that work is benchmarked against the preference-method families, and none of it measures internal representations. Unlike Gaps 1 and 2, this gap is about missing comparisons, not a missing method.

**What exists.** Our follow-up verification (raw report 06) found the RL-for-abstention line more populated than the initial gap analysis suggested. Reinforcement learning from knowledge feedback (RLKF) refusal training is the 2024 antecedent [arXiv:2403.18349], and the GRPO/verifiable-RL cluster now includes TruthRL [arXiv:2509.25760] (ternary +1/0/−1 reward in GRPO; on the CRAG retrieval benchmark with Llama3.1-8B-Instruct, hallucination 43.5% → 19.4% and truthfulness 5.3% → 37.2% vs. the prompting baseline), Abstain-R1 (SFT cold-start + GRPO with a clarification-aware reward on Qwen2.5-3B-Instruct, including an SFT-only ablation arm but no preference-method arm [arXiv:2604.17073]), reinforced hesitation's ternary +1/0/−λ reward [arXiv:2511.11500], fine-grained semantic-confidence rewards [arXiv:2510.24020], and the correctness-minus-Brier reward of reinforcement learning with calibration rewards (RLCR), which its authors report cutting calibration error by up to ~90% versus binary-reward RLVR at preserved accuracy [arXiv:2507.16806].

**What is still missing.** Two comparisons, specifically: none of these methods is benchmarked against SFT/DPO/KTO arms on a shared abstention dataset, and none measures internal representations. And the right comparison axis may be the reward rather than the method family: the AbstentionBench result that reasoning-RL *degrades* abstention by 24% [arXiv:2506.09038] shows the same method family helping or hurting depending on what the reward pays for. An observational recipe decomposition points the same way: released reasoning checkpoints trained with the full cold-start-SFT-plus-RL pipeline *improve* factuality over their non-reasoning counterparts (GLM-4-Z1-32B, TriviaQA 68.1 → 70.8) while RL-only and distillation-only checkpoints degrade it (same-base Qwen2.5-32B: 64.1 → 58.5 RL-only, 64.1 → 63.0 distillation-SFT), though recipe is confounded with training data in these checkpoint comparisons [arXiv:2505.23646].

**A caution for any study closing this gap.** Probes placed *inside* RL reward loops get gamed: GRPO with a lie-detector reward yields >85% deception, overwhelmingly detector-evading, once detector true-positive rate (TPR) falls below ~75%, while off-policy DPO stays under 25% at realistic TPRs [arXiv:2505.13787]. Representation probes must remain held-out evaluation, never reward.
#### Gap 4: No probe-transfer study tests whether humility fine-tuning changes representations or only behavior

**The claim** (high confidence; confirmed by the dedicated probing search, raw report 06). No paper trains epistemic probes on a base model, applies honesty-targeted fine-tuning, and re-reads the probes to ask whether the internal signal moved with the behavior.

**Why it matters.** Everything in Section 5 is behavioral: the corpus shows that training changes what models *say* about their knowledge, and says nothing about whether the representations underneath changed with it. The two are known to come apart: the surrogate-model result of Section 6.2 shows the token-level epistemic signal persisting in base weights while the stated signal is broken [arXiv:2311.08877]. That distinction is the difference between training humility and training the performance of humility, and probes are the only instrument currently available that can tell the two apart.

**Nearest existing evidence.** The probing toolkit is mature: linear decodability of truth/knowledge (Azaria-Mitchell internal-state probes at 71-83% accuracy [arXiv:2304.13734]; contrast-consistent search (CCS) [arXiv:2212.03827]; mass-mean truth probes with causal validation [arXiv:2310.06824]; inference-time intervention (ITI) raising TruthfulQA true*informative from 32.5% to 65.1% [arXiv:2306.03341]; representation-engineering contrast vectors [arXiv:2310.01405]), and pre-generation epistemic signals (P(IK), the model's predicted probability that it knows the answer [arXiv:2207.05221]; probes predicting semantic entropy [arXiv:2302.09664; Farquhar et al. 2024] at AUROC ~0.7-0.95, including from pre-generation hidden states [arXiv:2406.15927]). The closest prior art, "Fine-Tuned LLMs Know They Don't Know" [arXiv:2511.12991], shows base-trained probes retaining high AUROC after *ordinary task* SFT. But no paper runs this test across *honesty-targeted* fine-tuning, let alone across method families (SFT/KTO/GRPO).

**Cautions for the design.** Two probing-literature caveats bound the experiment: probes may read knowledge-recall rather than truth-tracking [arXiv:2510.09033], and cross-dataset probe transfer fails on negation-style shifts unless probes are trained on diverse data including negated statements [arXiv:2407.08582] (generalization also improves with model scale, a caution for our 3B setting [arXiv:2310.06824]). A credible experiment therefore needs diverse probe training sets and recall controls. In the image of Section 3, the open question is whether humility training tethers the statue or merely poses it.
#### Gap 5: No dose-response curve for IDK-example fraction in epistemic abstention

**The claim** (medium-high confidence). No study varies *how much* of an abstention training set says "I don't know" and traces the resulting curve from under-abstention to over-refusal.

**Why it matters.** Every abstention training set embeds a choice about its IDK fraction, and the literature currently makes that choice blind. If the safety analog below generalizes [arXiv:2309.07875], a small fraction does most of the work and an excess produces a model that refuses questions it could answer; without the epistemic version of the curve, no one can say where a given dataset sits on it.

**Nearest existing evidence.** The closest analog lives in the *safety*-refusal domain: Bianchi et al. add 100 → 2,000 safety instructions to 20k Alpaca examples (LLaMA-7B/13B, Falcon-7B) and find that ~3% safety data substantially reduces harmfulness while an excess induces "exaggerated safety", refusal of safe prompts that superficially resemble unsafe ones [arXiv:2309.07875]. That curve's over-refusal evidence is figure/annotation-based, and no equivalent sweep exists for the fraction of IDK examples in abstention training, so the gap stands. R-Tuning successors vary *which* questions become IDK, never *how many*, and report no abstention-precision/over-refusal Pareto curve.
#### Gap 6: Small-model and OOD-transfer coverage is thin

**The claim.** Nearly all training evidence sits at 7B+ parameters, and no paper measures whether preference-trained abstention survives a domain shift.

**Why it matters.** These are the two robustness questions a practitioner would ask first. Whether abstention-as-meta-skill [arXiv:2311.09677] holds at 1-3B is untested (the lone small-model entry, Abstain-R1 on Qwen2.5-3B, is SFT-cold-start + GRPO with no preference-method arm [arXiv:2604.17073]), and a trained behavior that holds only in its training domain is a much weaker result than the headline numbers suggest, so transfer evidence is part of the claim rather than an optional extension of it.

**Nearest existing evidence.** Cross-domain transfer of *trained* humility is positive but SFT-only and inconsistent (R-Tuning OOD; calibration-tuning transfer with ~1,000 graded examples sufficing [arXiv:2406.08391]; EliCal's 1k-annotation near-optimality [arXiv:2510.17509]; cross-domain SFT gains [arXiv:2510.05126]); no paper measures whether DPO- or KTO-trained abstention transfers out of domain, and the metric zoo (AP, prudence, truthful rate, AED, abstention recall) prevents cross-study comparison even where transfer is measured.

### 6.4 Positioning

Two existing surveys map adjacent territory: Wen et al.'s survey of abstention methods [arXiv:2407.18418] and Li et al.'s survey of LLM honesty [arXiv:2409.18786]. Both are narrative surveys, and both offer breadth this synthesis does not attempt. The comparison below is about division of labor, not deficiency.

**What the surveys cover.** Wen et al. organize abstention methods by pipeline stage (pretraining, alignment, inference) and catalogue inference-time approaches (prompting, consistency sampling, multi-LLM collaboration) that fall outside our training-effects scope. Li et al. organize honesty work along a self-knowledge versus self-expression divide, and cover decoding-time interventions, probing, and evaluation formalisms in depth.

**How this synthesis differs.** The differences are methodological rather than topical. Neither survey documents a search protocol, extracts effects into a comparable quantitative schema, verifies cited values against primary artifacts, or pools results across studies; those four steps are this synthesis's method (Section 4).

**What the method makes visible.** Both surveys mention trade-offs around over-abstention qualitatively, but neither examines the central tension this synthesis quantifies (preference training improving abstention and factuality while damaging calibration). Neither surfaces the method gaps of Section 6.3: KTO appears in neither survey, no three-way SFT/DPO/KTO comparison is noted as missing, and the IDK-fraction dose-response question is not raised.

**The relationship is complementary.** The surveys map the method space; this synthesis measures what the training side of that space has been shown to do, with per-row verification, and organizes the evidence by formalization depth rather than method family.

## 7. Limitations

**Extraction error.** Every quoted value in the corpus was checked against its primary PDF or artifact (June 2026): 73 of 75 rows (36 of 38 studies) are verified, and the eight rows admitted by the backward-citation pass were extracted directly from their primary PDFs at admission. Verification changed the corpus: six rows were corrected (a mislabeled metric, a mis-attributed model size, two values re-anchored from abstract claims to the papers' own tables, a re-identified baseline, and one reinterpreted comparison), and one row was excluded as a citation mis-attribution and removed from the corpus (its full record is preserved in the exclusion log, `meta-analysis/evidence/prisma-flow.md`).

That correction-plus-exclusion rate (7 of the 49 retrospectively checked rows, ~14%) is in line with what the methods literature measures for unverified single-pass extraction: single extraction produces measurably more errors than dual extraction (Buscemi et al. 2006), and GPT-4-assisted review extraction has been benchmarked at roughly 80% accuracy (Khraisha et al. 2024). This is why verification status is reported per row rather than asserted at the corpus level. The two rows still unverified (the nostalgebraist blog replication; a journal article without an accessible PDF, JMIR e76048) are flagged where cited and excluded from headline claims and family votes.

What verification cannot fix is omission. Confirming that every extracted number is right says nothing about the numbers that were never extracted: results a first reading passed over, and papers the searches never surfaced at all. The next two limitations take up that exposure.

**Single-extractor bias.** Each raw report was produced by one search agent and each effects row by one extractor; there is no double extraction and no inter-rater agreement statistic. Relatedly, verification and coverage are different guarantees: every number in the corpus is now checked against its primary source, but a single search pass cannot establish that no relevant paper went unfound, and the flow accounting of Section 4.1 therefore starts at structured assessment rather than at the records-identified count the PRISMA 2020 flow standard expects (Page et al. 2021), a disclosed deviation forced by the agentic search design. The backward-citation pass of Section 4.1 partially closes, and incidentally measures, this exposure: crawling the reference lists of every cited paper surfaced four admissible effect studies the forward searches had missed (all four now in the corpus) plus nineteen context citations, a concrete floor on forward-search leakage. The claim families were articulated after seeing the raw reports, so C1-C5 are confirmatory in form but exploratory in origin; the sign tests should be read descriptively.

**Coverage.** English-language papers only; arXiv-centric with two gray-literature items admitted by exception (Section 4.2). A five-language probe (Chinese, Japanese, Korean, French, German; June 2026) found surveys, detection methods, and inference-time mitigation work in native-language venues but no original quantitative training-intervention study on these outcomes published only outside English-language venues, consistent with this subfield publishing on arXiv in English regardless of lab origin; CNKI/Wanfang theses and non-archival Japanese NLP Society proceedings could not be searched directly and remain unscreened. A parallel venue probe (ACL Anthology, OpenReview, clinical journals, Nature-family, HCI venues; June 2026) confirmed the arXiv-centric pipeline leaks a small identifiable set of in-scope studies, concentrated in Anthology papers from groups that do not post preprints and in journal-first clinical informatics work; the confirmed cases are logged as v1 admission candidates in the evidence files, and none plausibly flips a pooled conclusion, though clinical fine-tuning studies are systematically undercounted by this design. Publication and visibility bias is uncorrected, and a literature this young likely has a file drawer of null interventions that vote counting cannot see. The search cutoff is June 2026; the L2-L4 levels of the framework rest on a small 2025-2026 cluster (all IDs and quoted values verified against PDFs, June 2026).

**Construct breadth.** "Epistemic humility" as operationalized here inherits the literature's L1 bias (Section 5.5): our synthesis quantifies what the field measured, which is mostly scalar confidence and flat refusal. The framework names what is missing, but the corpus cannot fill it.

**Reflexivity.** This synthesis is an instance of its own subject matter. Portions of the search, extraction, and verification pipeline were executed by LLM-based agents (Section 4.6), and their failure modes during this project were precisely the ones the corpus documents: numbers asserted confidently from snippets that did not support them, two figure-only values misread as though they had appeared in text, and one fluent mis-attribution of a real finding to the wrong paper (Section 6.3, gap 5). The verification protocol of Section 4.5 is the mechanism this literature trains into models (assert what is checked, flag the rest) applied to our own toolchain; the ~14% correction rate reported above is what it caught. A paper arguing that systems should admit what they do not know should expect to be judged by the same standard, and we have tried to make that judgment easy.

## 8. Future work: a staged program toward coherent humility

The synthesis points at one experiment-shaped question more insistently than any other. The unreconciled tension (Section 6.1), the estimator-fragility problem (Section 6.2), and Gaps 1-4 (Section 6.3) are facets of a single unknown: when fine-tuning makes a model behave humbly, does anything change beneath the behavior? We call the target *coherent humility*: a model whose intellectual humility agrees across its tokens, its hidden states, and its stated confidence, with that agreement verified mechanistically rather than inferred from behavior. The stakes of the distinction are practical. A model whose stated uncertainty verifiably tracks its internal state is auditable; one whose humility is performed is a polite liar with better manners.

The gaps of Section 6.3 build on one another rather than standing alone. The method comparison (Gaps 1 and 2: KTO has never been tried for humility training, and no study compares SFT, DPO, and KTO on shared abstention data) produces trained checkpoints. The mechanism study (Gap 4: it is untested whether humility training changes representations or only behavior) probes those same checkpoints. And the probes' answer determines what the generalization program (Gap 6: little evidence below 7B parameters or out of domain) should measure across other models. What follows is therefore a four-phase program rather than a list of disconnected experiments. The staged protocol, including hypotheses, falsifiers, and run matrices, is maintained in the project repository (`experiment/protocol/`), and each phase is pre-registered there before its first training run.

### 8.1 Phase 1: the three-way method comparison (Gaps 1 and 2)

**The experiment.** SFT, DPO, and KTO trained on the same base model, the same model-specific known/unknown split, and the same data budget: a single design that fills Gap 1 (the first KTO-for-abstention result) and Gap 2 (the first three-way comparison) at once.

**The measurement plan follows from Section 5.** Every arm is scored on the panel the literature splits across papers: refusal recall and over-refusal together (the decomposition of Section 5.3), truthful rate, token-level ECE after the same run (the first measurement of the Section 6.1 tension within a single training run, with KTO so far unmeasured on either side of it), and out-of-domain transfer, which no preference-trained abstention study has reported (Gap 6).

**The design hygiene follows from Sections 4 and 7.** Seed-level confidence intervals, where the calibration literature reports none; a pre-registered default configuration, with sensitivity panels that demonstrate robustness but can never supply a headline number; known/unknown labels regenerated for the model under study rather than borrowed, since the splits are model-specific by construction, with a label-noise sensitivity analysis motivated by the 42.9–51.3% noise rate Section 5.3 measured in released labels; and a replication bridge that reproduces Cheng et al.'s published numbers [arXiv:2401.13275] with the same pipeline before any novel arm is trusted.

### 8.2 Phase 2: data composition and the first epistemic dose-response curve (Gap 5)

Phase 1 asks which training method works best; Phase 2 asks how the training *data* should be built, holding the best Phase 1 method fixed. It varies one property of the data at a time, in three sub-experiments.

**The dose-response sweep (Gap 5).** No study varies what fraction of an abstention training set says "I don't know." Phase 2 sweeps that fraction and measures both failure modes at each setting: missed abstentions on questions the model cannot answer, and refusals of questions it can. The deliverable is the trade-off curve a practitioner currently has to guess at: choose a tolerable over-refusal rate, read off the IDK fraction that buys it. The only existing analog is Bianchi et al.'s safety-refusal curve, where roughly 3% safety data did most of the work and an excess made the model refuse harmless prompts [arXiv:2309.07875]; whether epistemic abstention behaves the same way is unknown.

**The composition test.** The two reanalyses of Section 5.3 point at a confound. In the Cheng setting, where fine-tuning data is abstention-targeted, SFT over-refused badly; in the Tulu-3 ladder, where SFT is a general instruction mix, precision differences across methods washed out. That contrast suggests over-refusal may follow from how concentrated the abstention data is rather than from the training method. The test: train on the same abstention examples twice, once on their own and once diluted into a general instruction mix, and see whether the over-refusal appears with the concentration or with the method.

**The balance ablation (KTO only).** KTO's training data is unpaired: each example is a single response labeled desirable or undesirable, so the ratio between the two kinds is a free choice. Paired-preference methods have no such choice, since every training item contains one of each by construction. The ablation varies that ratio and tests whether it moves the over-refusal operating point monotonically. If it does, KTO offers something no other method in the comparison has: a tunable dial for how cautious the trained model is.

### 8.3 Phase 3: mechanism (Gap 4)

Phase 3 takes up Gap 4: no study tests whether humility fine-tuning changes a model's internal representations or only its behavior. The test runs on the checkpoints Phase 1 produces. Linear probes for the model's knowledge state (P(IK)-style probes of whether the model will answer correctly [arXiv:2207.05221]) are trained on the base model's hidden states and re-read on each fine-tuned model. All three possible outcomes are informative. If behavior and hidden states move together, humility was internalized: coherence increased. If behavior moves over unchanged hidden states and the base probe still transfers, training produced a surface policy on an unchanged epistemic state: humility performed. If the probe stops transferring, fine-tuning moved the representation itself, and per-layer probes localize where. A data-side companion to this mechanism test is to audit the SFT/DPO/KTO training files themselves for concept-level signals before and after construction, following the preference-data audit logic of Bergen et al. [arXiv:2606.12360]. The design respects the cautions Gaps 3 and 4 collected: probes remain held-out evaluation and never enter a reward loop [arXiv:2505.13787], probe training sets are diverse enough to survive negation-style shifts [arXiv:2407.08582], and recall controls separate knowledge-recall from truth-tracking [arXiv:2510.09033].

### 8.4 Phase 4: generalization as a rolling program (Gap 6)

The first three phases are deliberately single-model-family; Phase 4 makes the pipeline the product. Re-running the full chain (model-specific labels, three-way training, the complete metric panel) across architectures and scales turns Gap 6's coverage complaint (training evidence concentrated at 7B and above, transfer unmeasured for preference methods) into a rolling result, and each re-run releases its harness, per-model labels, and outputs. The reanalyses of Section 5.3 were possible only because a minority of authors released theirs; this program defaults the other way.

Two further axes ride on the same harness. The first is sycophancy. Sharma et al., the source of the capitulation findings in Sections 1 and 5.4, measure sycophancy by re-asking the same question under mechanical pressure framings: the user offers no opinion, suggests the correct answer, suggests an incorrect one, or expresses doubt after a correct response [arXiv:2310.13548]. Applying those framings to questions the pipeline has already labeled known or unknown makes it possible to ask what the existing corpus cannot: does capitulation concentrate at the knowledge frontier, the questions at the edge of what the model demonstrably knows? Sycophancy and knowledge-boundary evaluations have so far been run on disjoint data, so that join does not yet exist anywhere. And a reasoning-trace axis re-runs the evaluation suite with explicit reasoning toggled on and off on the same model, asking whether a visible trace moves the knowledge frontier or relaxes the abstention-calibration tension; the finding that reasoning-oriented RL degrades abstention by 24% [arXiv:2506.09038], alongside the recipe-dependent factuality effects of reasoning training [arXiv:2505.23646], makes this axis more than a curiosity.

### 8.5 The standard the program inherits

The thread through all four phases is the measurement discipline this synthesis argues for. Each phase reports an estimator panel rather than a single metric (Section 6.2), so the primary quantity of any training comparison is its coherence delta: whether the token-level, hidden-state, and stated signals moved together. And each phase holds itself to the evidentiary standard this paper applies to its sources: pre-registered claims with falsifiers, per-row verification, and released artifacts, so that the next iteration of this synthesis can extract its rows without needing a correction pass.
