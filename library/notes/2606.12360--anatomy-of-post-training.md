## Abstract

Language-model post-training is the main stage at which model behavior is shaped, yet it still largely involves optimization of scalar rewards that summarize diverse desiderata. This abstraction gives practitioners little visibility into what their data actually teaches models, allowing spurious correlations to be learned by a model and inducing undesirable behaviors such as over-stylization and sycophancy. To address this problem, the paper introduces a data-centric post-training pipeline that uses interpretability protocols to develop statistical hypotheses for latent concepts separating preferred from dispreferred generations, then exposes those concepts for fine-grained user feedback. The paper unifies several interpretability-based training protocols as feature or data interventions for shaping rewards, and reports case studies where the pipeline diagnoses undesirable signals in preference data, mitigates off-target learning, and amplifies desired properties such as safeguards and model personality.

## Summary

The paper reframes post-training as auditing and sculpting the learning signal rather than optimizing an opaque scalar reward. Preference data is treated as a contrastive object: chosen and rejected completions are analyzed with SAE-feature clusters and related probes to identify concepts that distinguish high-reward from low-reward outputs. Those concepts can then be "explained away" or amplified through data filtering, inoculation prompting, activation steering, or reward shaping.

The most relevant empirical result for this project is not an abstention or calibration effect, but a diagnosis of how DPO can amplify off-target behaviors in realistic preference data. On Dolci-style DPO, the paper reports safety degradation relative to the SFT checkpoint, prompt-conditioned physics sycophancy, benchmark-identification behavior, and hallucinated sensitive-resource URLs. Reward shaping and filtering can partly move these metrics back toward the desired direction, but sparse or compositional behaviors such as sycophancy remain hard to modulate cleanly.

## Relevance to experiment

This paper strengthens the rationale for treating preference-training data as a source of latent behavioral signals rather than a neutral container of labels. For the Phase 1 SFT/DPO/KTO abstention comparison, it does not change the pre-registered design: it does not test KTO, IDK training, or token-level ECE. For Phase 3 and later mechanism work, it is directly useful: it gives a concrete neighboring framework for auditing whether a preference method has learned an intended concept, an off-target correlate, or a surface policy over an unchanged representation.
